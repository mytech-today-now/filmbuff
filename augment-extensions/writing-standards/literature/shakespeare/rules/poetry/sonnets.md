# Shakespearean Sonnets

## Overview

The Shakespearean sonnet is a 14-line poem in iambic pentameter with a specific rhyme scheme and thematic structure. Shakespeare wrote 154 sonnets, exploring themes of love, beauty, time, mortality, and art's power to immortalize.

## Sonnet Form

### Structure

**14 Lines Total**:
- **Three quatrains** (4 lines each)
- **One couplet** (2 lines)

**Rhyme Scheme**: **ABAB CDCD EFEF GG**

```
Quatrain 1: A B A B  (Lines 1-4)
Quatrain 2: C D C D  (Lines 5-8)
Quatrain 3: E F E F  (Lines 9-12)
Couplet:    G G      (Lines 13-14)
```

**Meter**: Iambic pentameter (10 syllables per line, unstressed/stressed pattern)

```
u / | u / | u / | u / | u /
(5 iambs = 10 syllables)
```

### Example: Sonnet 18 Structure

```
Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?        A
Thou art more lovely and more temperate:       B
Rough winds do shake the darling buds of May,  A
And summer's lease hath all too short a date:  B

Sometime too hot the eye of heaven shines,     C
And often is his gold complexion dimm'd;       D
And every fair from fair sometime declines,    C
By chance or nature's changing course untrimm'd; D

But thy eternal summer shall not fade          E
Nor lose possession of that fair thou owest;   F
Nor shall Death brag thou wander'st in his shade, E
When in eternal lines to time thou growest:    F

   So long as men can breathe or eyes can see,  G
   So long lives this and this gives life to thee. G
```

## Thematic Structure

### Quatrain 1 (Lines 1-4): Introduction
- **Purpose**: Introduce the topic, problem, or question
- **Function**: Establish the central concern or comparison
- **Example**: "Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?" (Sonnet 18)

### Quatrain 2 (Lines 5-8): Development
- **Purpose**: Develop, complicate, or explore the topic
- **Function**: Add depth, contrast, or additional perspectives
- **Example**: Explores summer's imperfections (too hot, too short, fades)

### Quatrain 3 (Lines 9-12): Volta (Turn)
- **Purpose**: Shift in perspective, argument, or tone
- **Function**: Introduce the "But..." moment that changes direction
- **Example**: "But thy eternal summer shall not fade" (Sonnet 18)

### Couplet (Lines 13-14): Resolution
- **Purpose**: Conclude, resolve, or deliver a twist
- **Function**: Provide insight, answer, or memorable closing
- **Example**: Poetry grants immortality - "So long lives this..."

## The Volta (Turn)

### Definition
The **volta** is the thematic or argumentative turn that typically occurs at line 9 (beginning of Quatrain 3).

### Common Volta Patterns

**1. Contradiction ("But...")**
```
Quatrains 1-2: Establish a problem or limitation
Quatrain 3: "But" introduces a solution or exception
Example: Sonnet 18 - Summer fades, BUT your beauty won't
```

**2. Realization ("Yet...")**
```
Quatrains 1-2: Describe a situation
Quatrain 3: "Yet" reveals deeper understanding
Example: Sonnet 73 - I'm aging, YET this makes love stronger
```

**3. Shift in Focus**
```
Quatrains 1-2: External description
Quatrain 3: Internal reflection or consequence
Example: Sonnet 130 - Realistic description, THEN true love declaration
```

### Volta Placement

**Traditional (Line 9)**:
- Most common in Shakespearean sonnets
- Marks beginning of Quatrain 3
- Often signaled by "But," "Yet," "Still," "Now"

**Early Volta (Line 5)**:
- Less common, creates two-part structure
- Quatrain 1 vs. Quatrains 2-3

**Late Volta (Line 13)**:
- Volta delayed to the couplet
- Creates surprise or twist ending
- All three quatrains build to final revelation

## Common Themes

### 1. Time and Mortality
**Description**: The passage of time, aging, death, and decay

**Examples**:
- Sonnet 18: "And summer's lease hath all too short a date"
- Sonnet 73: "That time of year thou mayst in me behold"
- Sonnet 12: "When I do count the clock that tells the time"

**Approach**: Often uses seasonal metaphors, clocks, decay imagery

### 2. Beauty and Transience
**Description**: Physical beauty fades, but can be preserved through art

**Examples**:
- Sonnet 18: "Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?"
- Sonnet 54: "O, how much more doth beauty beauteous seem"
- Sonnet 106: "When in the chronicle of wasted time"

**Approach**: Contrast between fleeting beauty and eternal verse

### 3. Love and Devotion
**Description**: Romantic love, constancy, devotion despite flaws

**Examples**:
- Sonnet 116: "Let me not to the marriage of true minds"
- Sonnet 130: "My mistress' eyes are nothing like the sun"
- Sonnet 29: "When, in disgrace with fortune and men's eyes"

**Approach**: Explores what true love means, often through paradox

### 4. Art and Immortality
**Description**: Poetry's power to preserve and immortalize

**Examples**:
- Sonnet 18: "So long lives this and this gives life to thee"
- Sonnet 55: "Not marble, nor the gilded monuments"
- Sonnet 81: "Your monument shall be my gentle verse"

**Approach**: Poetry outlasts physical monuments and time itself

### 5. Jealousy and Betrayal
**Description**: Suspicion, infidelity, emotional pain

**Examples**:
- Sonnet 144: "Two loves I have of comfort and despair"
- Sonnet 138: "When my love swears that she is made of truth"
- Sonnet 35: "No more be grieved at that which thou hast done"

**Approach**: Explores dark emotions, often with self-awareness

### 6. Self-Reflection and Identity
**Description**: Introspection, self-worth, reputation

**Examples**:
- Sonnet 29: "When, in disgrace with fortune and men's eyes"
- Sonnet 62: "Sin of self-love possesseth all mine eye"
- Sonnet 121: "'Tis better to be vile than vile esteemed"

**Approach**: Personal meditation on one's place in the world

## Rhyme Scheme Variations

### Standard Shakespearean (ABAB CDCD EFEF GG)
**Most Common**: Used in majority of Shakespeare's 154 sonnets

**Characteristics**:
- Alternating rhymes in quatrains
- Rhyming couplet conclusion
- Clear structural divisions

**Example**: Sonnet 18, 29, 73, 116, 130

### Occasional Variations

**1. Near Rhyme (Slant Rhyme)**
- Words that almost rhyme but not perfectly
- Example: "love" / "prove" (Sonnet 116)
- **Purpose**: Avoid forced rhymes, maintain natural language

**2. Eye Rhyme**
- Words that look like they rhyme but don't sound alike
- Example: "love" / "move" (pronunciation changed since Shakespeare's time)
- **Note**: Many were true rhymes in Elizabethan pronunciation

**3. Feminine Rhyme**
- Multi-syllable rhymes with unstressed final syllable
- Example: "pleasure" / "treasure"
- **Effect**: Softer, more lyrical quality

## Writing Techniques

### 1. Metaphor and Conceit
**Extended Metaphor**: Develop one central comparison throughout the sonnet

**Example - Sonnet 73**:
- Quatrain 1: Autumn metaphor (aging as late fall)
- Quatrain 2: Twilight metaphor (aging as approaching night)
- Quatrain 3: Dying fire metaphor (aging as embers fading)
- **Effect**: Progressive intensification of mortality theme

### 2. Paradox and Oxymoron
**Paradox**: Seemingly contradictory statement that reveals truth

**Examples**:
- "Love is not love which alters when it alteration finds" (Sonnet 116)
- "My mistress' eyes are nothing like the sun" (yet I love her) (Sonnet 130)

**Effect**: Challenges conventional thinking, reveals deeper truth

### 3. Personification
**Technique**: Give human qualities to abstract concepts or nature

**Examples**:
- "Death shall not brag" (Sonnet 18)
- "Time's fell hand" (Sonnet 64)
- "Love is not Time's fool" (Sonnet 116)

**Effect**: Makes abstract concepts tangible and dramatic

### 4. Imagery and Sensory Detail
**Technique**: Engage multiple senses to create vivid pictures

**Example - Sonnet 73**:
- **Visual**: "Yellow leaves, or none, or few"
- **Auditory**: "Where late the sweet birds sang"
- **Tactile**: "Shake against the cold"
- **Thermal**: "Glowing of such fire"

**Effect**: Makes abstract themes concrete and emotionally resonant

### 5. Repetition and Anaphora
**Anaphora**: Repeating words at the beginning of lines or clauses

**Example - Sonnet 66**:
```
Tired with all these, for restful death I cry,
As, to behold desert a beggar born,
And needy nothing trimm'd in jollity,
And purest faith unhappily forsworn,
And gilded honour shamefully misplaced...
```

**Effect**: Creates rhythm, emphasizes accumulation, builds intensity

## Inline Examples

### Example 1: Sonnet 116 (Love's Constancy)

```
Let me not to the marriage of true minds
Admit impediments. Love is not love
Which alters when it alteration finds,
Or bends with the remover to remove:
O no! it is an ever-fixed mark
That looks on tempests and is never shaken;
It is the star to every wandering bark,
Whose worth's unknown, although his height be taken.
Love's not Time's fool, though rosy lips and cheeks
Within his bending sickle's compass come:
Love alters not with his brief hours and weeks,
But bears it out even to the edge of doom.
   If this be error and upon me proved,
   I never writ, nor no man ever loved.
```

**Analysis**:
- **Quatrain 1**: Defines what love is NOT (doesn't change)
- **Quatrain 2**: Metaphor - love as fixed star guiding ships
- **Quatrain 3**: Love transcends time and physical decay
- **Couplet**: Bold assertion - if I'm wrong, nothing I wrote is true

**Rhyme Scheme**: ABAB CDCD EFEF GG ✓
**Meter**: Iambic pentameter with strategic variations
**Theme**: True love is constant and unchanging
**Volta**: Line 9 - "Love's not Time's fool" (shift from definition to assertion)

### Example 2: Sonnet 29 (Finding Solace in Love)

```
When, in disgrace with fortune and men's eyes,
I all alone beweep my outcast state
And trouble deaf heaven with my bootless cries
And look upon myself and curse my fate,
Wishing me like to one more rich in hope,
Featured like him, like him with friends possess'd,
Desiring this man's art and that man's scope,
With what I most enjoy contented least;
Yet in these thoughts myself almost despising,
Haply I think on thee, and then my state,
Like to the lark at break of day arising
From sullen earth, sings hymns at heaven's gate;
   For thy sweet love remember'd such wealth brings
   That then I scorn to change my state with kings.
```

**Analysis**:
- **Quatrains 1-2**: Deep depression, self-pity, envy of others
- **Quatrain 3**: Volta at "Yet" - thinking of beloved transforms mood
- **Couplet**: Love makes speaker richer than kings

**Rhyme Scheme**: ABAB CDCD EFEF GG ✓
**Theme**: Love as redemption from despair
**Volta**: Line 9 - "Yet in these thoughts" (dramatic emotional shift)
**Imagery**: Lark rising from earth to heaven (despair to joy)

## Practical Application

### Writing Your Own Sonnet

**Step 1: Choose Your Theme**
- Select from common themes (love, time, beauty, mortality)
- Or explore a personal topic through Shakespearean lens

**Step 2: Plan Your Structure**
- **Lines 1-4**: Introduce your topic or question
- **Lines 5-8**: Develop or complicate the topic
- **Lines 9-12**: Introduce the volta (turn)
- **Lines 13-14**: Resolve with a memorable couplet

**Step 3: Draft Without Worrying About Form**
- Write your ideas in prose first
- Focus on what you want to say

**Step 4: Shape Into Iambic Pentameter**
- Count syllables (aim for 10 per line)
- Read aloud to check natural stress pattern
- Adjust word choice to fit meter

**Step 5: Add Rhymes**
- Work on end rhymes (ABAB CDCD EFEF GG)
- Use near rhymes if perfect rhymes feel forced
- Prioritize meaning over perfect rhyme

**Step 6: Revise and Polish**
- Check that volta is clear and effective
- Ensure couplet provides satisfying conclusion
- Read aloud multiple times
- Refine imagery and word choice

### Common Pitfalls to Avoid

**1. Forced Rhymes**
- **Problem**: Choosing words only because they rhyme
- **Solution**: Use near rhymes or rework the line entirely

**2. Inverted Syntax**
- **Problem**: "The ball red I threw" (unnatural word order for rhyme)
- **Solution**: Rework the line to maintain natural speech

**3. Weak Couplet**
- **Problem**: Couplet feels tacked on or doesn't resolve the sonnet
- **Solution**: Make the couplet your strongest, most memorable lines

**4. Unclear Volta**
- **Problem**: No clear turn or shift in the poem
- **Solution**: Use signal words ("But," "Yet," "Still") and ensure thematic shift

**5. Meter Over Meaning**
- **Problem**: Sacrificing clarity for perfect meter
- **Solution**: Meaning first, then adjust meter; strategic variations are acceptable

## Summary Checklist

When writing or analyzing a Shakespearean sonnet, verify:

- [ ] **14 lines** total
- [ ] **Rhyme scheme**: ABAB CDCD EFEF GG
- [ ] **Meter**: Iambic pentameter (10 syllables per line)
- [ ] **Quatrain 1** (1-4): Introduces topic
- [ ] **Quatrain 2** (5-8): Develops topic
- [ ] **Quatrain 3** (9-12): Volta (turn)
- [ ] **Couplet** (13-14): Resolution
- [ ] **Clear volta** at line 9 (or 5, or 13)
- [ ] **Strong couplet** that resolves or surprises
- [ ] **Unified theme** throughout
- [ ] **Vivid imagery** and sensory detail
- [ ] **Natural language** despite formal constraints

---

**Remember**: The sonnet form is a tool for exploring complex ideas and emotions. Master the form, then use it to express genuine thoughts and feelings. Shakespeare's sonnets endure not because of perfect meter, but because of profound human insight expressed through disciplined artistry.
