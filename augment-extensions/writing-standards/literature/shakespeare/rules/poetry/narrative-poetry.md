# Narrative Poetry

A comprehensive guide to Shakespearean narrative poetry, exploring structure, techniques, and character development through verse.

## Overview

Shakespeare's narrative poems tell complete stories through verse, combining poetic techniques with narrative structure. His major narrative works—*Venus and Adonis* (1593) and *The Rape of Lucrece* (1594)—demonstrate sophisticated storytelling through poetry, blending vivid imagery, psychological depth, and formal verse structures.

## Structure of Narrative Poems

### Venus and Adonis

**Form**: 1,194 lines in 199 six-line stanzas

**Stanza Form**: Sestet (six-line stanza)

**Rhyme Scheme**: ABABCC

**Meter**: Iambic pentameter

**Structure**:
```
Line 1: A (10 syllables, iambic pentameter)
Line 2: B (10 syllables, iambic pentameter)
Line 3: A (10 syllables, iambic pentameter)
Line 4: B (10 syllables, iambic pentameter)
Line 5: C (10 syllables, iambic pentameter)
Line 6: C (10 syllables, iambic pentameter - couplet conclusion)
```

**Example Stanza**:
```
Even as the sun with purple-coloured face        (A)
Had ta'en his last leave of the weeping morn,    (B)
Rose-cheeked Adonis hied him to the chase;       (A)
Hunting he loved, but love he laughed to scorn.  (B)
Sick-thoughted Venus makes amain unto him,       (C)
And like a bold-faced suitor 'gins to woo him.   (C)
```

**Narrative Characteristics**:
- **Erotic comedy**: Venus pursues reluctant Adonis
- **Mythological subject**: Classical story retold
- **Sensual imagery**: Rich, physical descriptions
- **Psychological complexity**: Characters' inner conflicts
- **Tragic conclusion**: Adonis killed by boar

### The Rape of Lucrece

**Form**: 1,855 lines in 265 seven-line stanzas

**Stanza Form**: Rhyme royal (seven-line stanza)

**Rhyme Scheme**: ABABBCC

**Meter**: Iambic pentameter

**Structure**:
```
Line 1: A (10 syllables, iambic pentameter)
Line 2: B (10 syllables, iambic pentameter)
Line 3: A (10 syllables, iambic pentameter)
Line 4: B (10 syllables, iambic pentameter)
Line 5: B (10 syllables, iambic pentameter)
Line 6: C (10 syllables, iambic pentameter)
Line 7: C (10 syllables, iambic pentameter - couplet conclusion)
```

**Example Stanza**:
```
From the besieged Ardea all in post,              (A)
Borne by the trustless wings of false desire,     (B)
Lust-breathed Tarquin leaves the Roman host,      (A)
And to Collatium bears the lightless fire         (B)
Which, in pale embers hid, lurks to aspire        (B)
And girdle with embracing flames the waist        (C)
Of Collatine's fair love, Lucrece the chaste.     (C)
```

**Narrative Characteristics**:
- **Tragic subject**: Rape and suicide
- **Moral gravity**: Serious, philosophical tone
- **Psychological depth**: Extended internal monologues
- **Political implications**: Fall of Roman monarchy
- **Rhetorical complexity**: Elaborate arguments and debates

## Stanza Forms and Rhyme Schemes

### Sestet (Six-Line Stanza)

**Rhyme Scheme**: ABABCC

**Characteristics**:
- Four alternating rhyme lines (ABAB)
- Concluding couplet (CC)
- Couplet provides closure or commentary
- Suitable for lighter, faster-paced narrative

**Effect**:
- Quatrain develops idea or action
- Couplet delivers punchline, moral, or transition
- Creates rhythmic momentum
- Allows for wit and surprise

**Example - Venus and Adonis**:
```
"Fondling," she saith, "since I have hemmed thee here    (A)
Within the circuit of this ivory pale,                   (B)
I'll be a park, and thou shalt be my deer;               (A)
Feed where thou wilt, on mountain or in dale:            (B)
Graze on my lips; and if those hills be dry,             (C)
Stray lower, where the pleasant fountains lie."          (C)
```
(Quatrain sets up metaphor, couplet completes seduction)

### Rhyme Royal (Seven-Line Stanza)

**Rhyme Scheme**: ABABBCC

**Characteristics**:
- Five lines with two rhymes (ABABB)
- Concluding couplet (CC)
- More complex than sestet
- Suitable for serious, elevated subjects

**Effect**:
- Extended development before resolution
- Greater dignity and weight
- Allows for philosophical reflection
- Creates stately, measured pace

**Example - The Rape of Lucrece**:
```
What win I if I gain the thing I seek?                   (A)
A dream, a breath, a froth of fleeting joy.              (B)
Who buys a minute's mirth to wail a week?                (A)
Or sells eternity to get a toy?                          (B)
For one sweet grape who will the vine destroy?           (B)
Or what fond beggar, but to touch the crown,             (C)
Would with the sceptre straight be strucken down?        (C)
```
(Extended rhetorical questions build to couplet conclusion)

## Narrative Techniques in Verse

### 1. Vivid Sensory Description

**Purpose**: Bring scenes to life through rich imagery

**Technique**: Engage all five senses

**Example - Venus and Adonis**:
```
Full gently now she takes him by the hand,
A lily prisoned in a jail of snow,
Or ivory in an alabaster band;
So white a friend engirts so white a foe.
```
(Visual and tactile imagery: whiteness, softness, contrast)

### 2. Direct Speech and Dialogue

**Purpose**: Reveal character, advance plot, create drama

**Technique**: Characters speak directly within narrative verse

**Example - Venus and Adonis**:
```
"I'll be a park, and thou shalt be my deer;
Feed where thou wilt, on mountain or in dale:
Graze on my lips; and if those hills be dry,
Stray lower, where the pleasant fountains lie."
```
(Venus's seductive speech reveals character and desire)

### 3. Internal Monologue

**Purpose**: Explore character psychology and moral conflict

**Technique**: Extended passages of character's thoughts

**Example - The Rape of Lucrece**:
```
"What win I if I gain the thing I seek?
A dream, a breath, a froth of fleeting joy.
Who buys a minute's mirth to wail a week?
Or sells eternity to get a toy?"
```
(Tarquin debates with himself before committing crime)

### 4. Narrator Commentary

**Purpose**: Guide reader interpretation, provide context

**Technique**: Narrator speaks directly about characters or events

**Example - The Rape of Lucrece**:
```
This said, he shakes aloft his Roman blade,
Which, like a falcon towering in the skies,
Coucheth the fowl below with his wings' shade,
Whose crooked beak threats if he mount he dies.
```
(Narrator describes action with epic simile)

### 5. Rhetorical Devices

**Purpose**: Enhance persuasive power, emotional impact

**Technique**: Use metaphor, simile, personification, apostrophe

**Example - Venus and Adonis**:
```
"Bid me discourse, I will enchant thine ear,
Or like a fairy trip upon the green,
Or like a nymph with long dishevelled hair
Dance on the sands, and yet no footing seen."
```
(Similes create mythological imagery)

### 6. Pacing and Tempo

**Purpose**: Control narrative speed and emotional intensity

**Technique**: Vary sentence length, use enjambment or end-stopping

**Fast Pace** (short sentences, end-stopped lines):
```
Lo, here the gentle lark, weary of rest,
From his moist cabinet mounts up on high
And wakes the morning, from whose silver breast
The sun ariseth in his majesty.
```

**Slow Pace** (long sentences, enjambment):
```
Even as an empty eagle, sharp by fast,
Tires with her beak on feathers, flesh, and bone,
Shaking her wings, devouring all in haste
Till either gorge be stuffed or prey be gone.
```

## Character Development Through Poetry

### Psychological Complexity

**Technique**: Use verse to explore character's inner life

**Example - Lucrece's Internal Conflict**:

**Before the Rape** (innocence):
```
Her breasts like ivory globes circled with blue,
A pair of maiden worlds unconquered,
Save of their lord no bearing yoke they knew,
And him by oath they truly honoured.
```

**After the Rape** (shame and despair):
```
"O comfort-killing Night, image of hell,
Dim register and notary of shame,
Black stage for tragedies and murders fell,
Vast sin-concealing chaos, nurse of blame!"
```

**Character Arc**: Innocence → Violation → Despair → Resolve → Tragic Death

### Contrasting Characters

**Technique**: Use different speech patterns and imagery for different characters

**Venus** (passionate, sensual):
```
"I'll be a park, and thou shalt be my deer;
Feed where thou wilt, on mountain or in dale."
```
(Natural, erotic imagery)

**Adonis** (cold, resistant):
```
"I know not love," quoth he, "nor will not know it,
Unless it be a boar, and then I chase it."
```
(Rejection, preference for hunting over love)

### Emotional Transformation

**Technique**: Show character change through evolving language and imagery

**Tarquin's Progression**:

**Stage 1 - Desire**:
```
His eye commends the leading to his hand;
His hand, as proud of such a dignity,
Smoking with pride, marched on to make his stand
On her bare breast, the heart of all her land.
```

**Stage 2 - Guilt**:
```
"What win I if I gain the thing I seek?
A dream, a breath, a froth of fleeting joy."
```

**Stage 3 - Shame**:
```
He like a thievish dog creeps sadly thence;
She like a wearied lamb lies panting there.
```

## Complete Example with Analysis

### From Venus and Adonis (Lines 229-234)

**Text**:
```
"Fondling," she saith, "since I have hemmed thee here    (A)
Within the circuit of this ivory pale,                   (B)
I'll be a park, and thou shalt be my deer;               (A)
Feed where thou wilt, on mountain or in dale:            (B)
Graze on my lips; and if those hills be dry,             (C)
Stray lower, where the pleasant fountains lie."          (C)
```

**Analysis**:

**Stanza Form**: Sestet (ABABCC)

**Narrative Function**: Venus attempts to seduce Adonis

**Character Revelation**:
- Venus is aggressive, sexually forward
- Uses hunting metaphor (ironic, as Adonis loves hunting)
- Transforms herself into landscape for his pleasure

**Poetic Techniques**:
- **Extended Metaphor**: Venus as park, Adonis as deer
- **Erotic Imagery**: "Graze on my lips," "pleasant fountains"
- **Wordplay**: "deer/dear" pun
- **Enjambment**: Lines 3-4 flow together
- **Couplet Conclusion**: Escalates seduction

**Rhyme and Rhythm**:
- Perfect rhymes: here/deer, pale/dale, dry/lie
- Iambic pentameter maintained
- Couplet provides emphatic conclusion

**Tone**: Playful, sensual, persuasive

**Effect**: Reader sees Venus's desire and Adonis's position as "prey"

## Practical Application

### Writing Narrative Poetry

**Step 1: Choose Your Form**

- **Sestet (ABABCC)**: Lighter subjects, faster pace
- **Rhyme Royal (ABABBCC)**: Serious subjects, elevated tone

**Step 2: Plan Your Narrative**

- Beginning: Establish characters and situation
- Middle: Develop conflict and action
- End: Resolve narrative (tragic or comic)

**Step 3: Develop Characters**

- Give each character distinctive voice
- Use imagery that reflects character
- Show psychological depth through internal monologue

**Step 4: Balance Poetry and Story**

- Don't sacrifice narrative clarity for poetic effect
- Use poetic devices to enhance story
- Maintain consistent meter and rhyme

**Step 5: Use Stanza Structure**

- Quatrain/quintet: Develop idea or action
- Couplet: Conclude, comment, or transition

**Step 6: Vary Techniques**

- Mix description, dialogue, and narration
- Alternate between external action and internal thought
- Use rhetorical devices purposefully

## Common Pitfalls

**Pitfall 1: Sacrificing Clarity for Rhyme**

❌ Awkward word order to force rhyme
✅ Natural syntax that maintains rhyme

**Pitfall 2: Monotonous Pacing**

❌ Every stanza same structure and rhythm
✅ Vary sentence length and enjambment

**Pitfall 3: Weak Couplets**

❌ Couplet feels tacked on or forced
✅ Couplet provides meaningful conclusion

**Pitfall 4: Neglecting Character**

❌ Beautiful poetry but flat characters
✅ Poetry reveals and develops character

**Pitfall 5: Losing Narrative Thread**

❌ So focused on poetic effect that story becomes unclear
✅ Poetic techniques serve narrative clarity

## Checklist for Narrative Poetry

When writing narrative poetry:

- [ ] Consistent stanza form throughout (sestet or rhyme royal)
- [ ] Maintains iambic pentameter (with purposeful variations)
- [ ] Rhyme scheme followed precisely
- [ ] Narrative is clear and engaging
- [ ] Characters have distinct voices
- [ ] Psychological depth through internal monologue
- [ ] Vivid sensory imagery
- [ ] Effective use of rhetorical devices
- [ ] Couplets provide meaningful conclusions
- [ ] Balance between poetry and storytelling
- [ ] Emotional arc for characters
- [ ] Satisfying narrative resolution
