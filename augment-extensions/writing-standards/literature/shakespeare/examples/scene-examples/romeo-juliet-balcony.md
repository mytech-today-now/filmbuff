# Romeo and Juliet: Balcony Scene (Act 2, Scene 2)

## Scene Context

**Play**: *Romeo and Juliet* (1594-1596)
**Act/Scene**: Act 2, Scene 2
**Location**: Capulet's orchard, beneath Juliet's balcony
**Time**: Night, immediately after the Capulet ball
**Characters**: Romeo Montague, Juliet Capulet

**Dramatic Situation**: Romeo has climbed into the Capulet orchard to catch a glimpse of Juliet. She appears on her balcony, unaware he's below, and speaks her thoughts aloud.

## Excerpt: Lines 1-49

```
ROMEO
He jests at scars that never felt a wound.

[JULIET appears above at a window]

But, soft! what light through yonder window breaks?
It is the east, and Juliet is the sun.
Arise, fair sun, and kill the envious moon,
Who is already sick and pale with grief,
That thou her maid art far more fair than she:
Be not her maid, since she is envious;
Her vestal livery is but sick and green
And none but fools do wear it; cast it off.
It is my lady, O, it is my love!
O, that she knew she were!
She speaks yet she says nothing: what of that?
Her eye discourses; I will answer it.
I am too bold, 'tis not to me she speaks:
Two of the fairest stars in all the heaven,
Having some business, do entreat her eyes
To twinkle in their spheres till they return.
What if her eyes were there, they in her head?
The brightness of her cheek would shame those stars,
As daylight doth a lamp; her eyes in heaven
Would through the airy region stream so bright
That birds would sing and think it were not night.
See, how she leans her cheek upon her hand!
O, that I were a glove upon that hand,
That I might touch that cheek!

JULIET
                                Ay me!

ROMEO
                                        She speaks:
O, speak again, bright angel! for thou art
As glorious to this night, being o'er my head
As is a winged messenger of heaven
Unto the white-upturned wondering eyes
Of mortals that fall back to gaze on him
When he bestrides the lazy-pacing clouds
And sails upon the bosom of the air.

JULIET
O Romeo, Romeo! wherefore art thou Romeo?
Deny thy father and refuse thy name;
Or, if thou wilt not, be but sworn my love,
And I'll no longer be a Capulet.

ROMEO
[Aside] Shall I hear more, or shall I speak at this?

JULIET
'Tis but thy name that is my enemy;
Thou art thyself, though not a Montague.
What's Montague? it is nor hand, nor foot,
Nor arm, nor face, nor any other part
Belonging to a man. O, be some other name!
What's in a name? that which we call a rose
By any other name would smell as sweet;
So Romeo would, were he not Romeo call'd,
Retain that dear perfection which he owes
Without that title. Romeo, doff thy name,
And for that name which is no part of thee
Take all myself.
```

## Character Analysis

### Romeo

**Characteristics**:
- **Passionate**: Speaks in elaborate metaphors and hyperbole
- **Poetic**: Uses extended conceits (Juliet as sun, angel, etc.)
- **Impulsive**: Climbs into enemy's orchard, risks death
- **Romantic idealist**: Sees Juliet in cosmic, divine terms

**Language**:
- **Metaphor-heavy**: Sun, moon, stars, angels, heaven
- **Elevated diction**: "yonder," "wherefore," "thou art"
- **Exclamations**: "O!", "soft!", expressing wonder and passion

**Character Arc in Scene**:
1. Awestruck observer (lines 2-25)
2. Hesitant eavesdropper (line 37: "Shall I hear more?")
3. Active participant (reveals himself later in scene)

### Juliet

**Characteristics**:
- **Practical**: Questions the importance of names
- **Philosophical**: "What's in a name?"
- **Bold**: Willing to renounce her family name
- **Honest**: Speaks her true feelings, unaware of audience

**Language**:
- **Direct questions**: "wherefore art thou Romeo?"
- **Logical argument**: Names are arbitrary, not essential
- **Concrete imagery**: Rose metaphor is simple, effective
- **Imperative mood**: "Deny thy father," "refuse thy name"

**Character Arc in Scene**:
1. Lamenting the obstacle (lines 33-35)
2. Reasoning through the problem (lines 38-49)
3. Offering solution (line 49: "Take all myself")

## Dramatic Techniques

### 1. Dramatic Irony
**Juliet doesn't know Romeo is listening**. The audience knows, creating tension and anticipation.

### 2. Soliloquy vs. Aside
- **Romeo's speeches**: Soliloquy (alone on stage, speaking thoughts)
- **Line 37**: Aside (brief comment to audience, Juliet doesn't hear)
- **Juliet's speeches**: Soliloquy (thinks she's alone)

### 3. Metaphorical Language
**Romeo's extended conceits**:
- **Juliet = Sun**: Brings light, life, warmth; outshines the moon (Diana, goddess of chastity)
- **Juliet = Angel**: Divine, heavenly, beyond mortal realm
- **Juliet's eyes = Stars**: Bright, celestial, guiding

### 4. Contrast
- **Romeo**: Elaborate, metaphorical, cosmic imagery
- **Juliet**: Direct, logical, practical reasoning
- **Effect**: Shows different approaches to same problem (their love)

### 5. Verse Form Variation
- **Regular iambic pentameter**: Calm, controlled moments
- **Spondees**: Emotional emphasis
- **Extended lines**: Heightened emotion (line 33)
- **Truncated lines**: Pauses, interruptions (line 25)

## Staging Notes

### Physical Staging

**Vertical Separation**:
- **Juliet**: Above, on balcony (elevated, unreachable, idealized)
- **Romeo**: Below, in orchard (earthbound, looking up, worshipful)
- **Symbolism**: Social barriers, class difference, aspiration

**Lighting**:
- **Night setting**: Darkness allows secrecy, intimacy
- **Moonlight**: Traditional romantic lighting
- **Juliet as light source**: She "breaks" through the window, illuminates Romeo's world

**Movement**:
- **Romeo**: Moves closer to balcony, gestures upward
- **Juliet**: Leans on balcony rail, looks at stars/moon (not down at Romeo yet)
- **Line 23**: "See, how she leans her cheek upon her hand!" - specific gesture

### Performance Notes

**Romeo**:
- **Tone**: Awestruck, reverent, passionate
- **Volume**: Starts quiet (hiding), builds with emotion
- **Pacing**: Rapid in excitement, pauses for wonder

**Juliet**:
- **Tone**: Frustrated (at situation), then determined
- **Volume**: Speaking to herself, moderate volume
- **Pacing**: Thoughtful, building to resolution

**Key Moments**:
- **Line 2**: "But, soft!" - sudden stop, whispered
- **Line 26**: "Ay me!" - Juliet's sigh, Romeo's reaction
- **Line 33**: "O Romeo, Romeo!" - anguished, questioning
- **Line 43**: "What's in a name?" - rhetorical, philosophical shift

## Thematic Elements

### 1. Love vs. Social Barriers
The scene establishes the central conflict: their love vs. family feud.

### 2. Names and Identity
**Juliet's question**: Are we defined by our names/families, or by our essential selves?

### 3. Light vs. Darkness
**Light** = love, truth, Juliet
**Darkness** = secrecy, danger, the feud

### 4. Heaven vs. Earth
**Romeo** sees Juliet in celestial terms (sun, angel, stars), elevating earthly love to divine.

### 5. Public vs. Private
The scene is intimate, private—contrasts with public world of feud and social expectations.

## Writing Lessons

### 1. Contrasting Character Voices
Romeo and Juliet speak differently, revealing character through language choice.

### 2. Dramatic Irony Creates Tension
Juliet's unawareness of Romeo's presence engages the audience.

### 3. Metaphor Reveals Character
Romeo's elaborate metaphors show his romantic, poetic nature.

### 4. Vertical Staging as Symbol
Physical space (high/low) reinforces thematic content (aspiration, barriers).

### 5. Verse Variation for Emotion
Metrical changes (spondees, extended lines) mark emotional peaks.

### 6. Soliloquy for Interiority
Characters speak their true thoughts, giving audience access to inner life.

## Practice Exercise

**Write a scene with vertical staging**:
1. Place one character above, one below (balcony, window, cliff, etc.)
2. Use the physical separation to symbolize emotional/social distance
3. Have one character unaware the other is listening (dramatic irony)
4. Contrast their language styles to reveal character
5. Use iambic pentameter with strategic variations

**Consider**:
- What does the height difference symbolize?
- How does the eavesdropping create tension?
- What do the different speech patterns reveal?
