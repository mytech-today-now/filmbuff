# Hamlet: "To be, or not to be" Soliloquy (Act 3, Scene 1)

## Scene Context

**Play**: *Hamlet* (1599-1601)
**Act/Scene**: Act 3, Scene 1
**Location**: A room in the castle
**Time**: After Hamlet has seen his father's ghost and learned of the murder
**Character**: Hamlet, Prince of Denmark

**Dramatic Situation**: Hamlet is contemplating suicide and the nature of existence. Claudius and Polonius are hiding nearby, spying on him. Ophelia will enter shortly.

## Full Soliloquy (Lines 56-90)

```
HAMLET
To be, or not to be: that is the question:
Whether 'tis nobler in the mind to suffer
The slings and arrows of outrageous fortune,
Or to take arms against a sea of troubles,
And by opposing end them? To die: to sleep;
No more; and by a sleep to say we end
The heart-ache and the thousand natural shocks
That flesh is heir to, 'tis a consummation
Devoutly to be wish'd. To die, to sleep;
To sleep: perchance to dream: ay, there's the rub;
For in that sleep of death what dreams may come
When we have shuffled off this mortal coil,
Must give us pause: there's the respect
That makes calamity of so long life;
For who would bear the whips and scorns of time,
The oppressor's wrong, the proud man's contumely,
The pangs of despised love, the law's delay,
The insolence of office and the spurns
That patient merit of the unworthy takes,
When he himself might his quietus make
With a bare bodkin? who would fardels bear,
To grunt and sweat under a weary life,
But that the dread of something after death,
The undiscover'd country from whose bourn
No traveller returns, puzzles the will
And makes us rather bear those ills we have
Than fly to others that we know not of?
Thus conscience does make cowards of us all;
And thus the native hue of resolution
Is sicklied o'er with the pale cast of thought,
And enterprises of great pith and moment
With this regard their currents turn awry,
And lose the name of action.—Soft you now!
The fair Ophelia! Nymph, in thy orisons
Be all my sins remember'd.
```

## Character Analysis

### Hamlet's State of Mind

**Characteristics in This Moment**:
- **Philosophical**: Contemplates abstract questions of existence
- **Depressed**: Considers suicide as escape from suffering
- **Analytical**: Weighs pros and cons methodically
- **Paralyzed**: Thought prevents action ("conscience does make cowards")
- **Isolated**: Speaks to himself, unaware of (or ignoring) eavesdroppers

**Psychological Journey**:
1. **Lines 1-5**: Poses the question - to live or die?
2. **Lines 5-10**: Death as peaceful sleep seems attractive
3. **Lines 10-14**: Fear of afterlife ("what dreams may come")
4. **Lines 15-22**: Catalog of life's sufferings
5. **Lines 23-27**: Fear of unknown prevents suicide
6. **Lines 28-32**: Conclusion - thinking paralyzes action
7. **Lines 33-35**: Sudden shift - notices Ophelia

### Language Patterns

**Abstract vs. Concrete**:
- **Abstract**: "to be," "question," "nobler," "fortune," "consummation"
- **Concrete**: "slings and arrows," "bare bodkin," "fardels," "grunt and sweat"
- **Effect**: Moves between philosophical abstraction and visceral reality

**Metaphors**:
- **Death = Sleep**: Extended metaphor throughout
- **Life = Sea of troubles**: Overwhelming, drowning
- **Afterlife = Undiscovered country**: Unknown territory
- **Thought = Disease**: "sicklied o'er with the pale cast of thought"

**Repetition**:
- "To be, or not to be"
- "To die, to sleep" (repeated 3 times)
- "To sleep, perchance to dream"
- Creates rhythm, emphasizes key concepts

## Dramatic Techniques

### 1. Soliloquy
**Function**: Reveals Hamlet's inner thoughts directly to audience
**Effect**: Creates intimacy, allows philosophical exploration
**Convention**: Other characters (Claudius, Polonius) don't hear, though they're present

### 2. Rhetorical Questions
- "Whether 'tis nobler..."
- "For who would bear the whips and scorns of time..."
- "who would fardels bear..."

**Effect**: Engages audience in the reasoning process

### 3. Catalog/List
Lines 15-19 list life's sufferings:
- "whips and scorns of time"
- "oppressor's wrong"
- "proud man's contumely"
- "pangs of despised love"
- "law's delay"
- "insolence of office"
- "spurns that patient merit of the unworthy takes"

**Effect**: Accumulates weight, justifies consideration of suicide

### 4. Antithesis
- **To be** vs. **not to be**
- **To suffer** vs. **to take arms**
- **To die** vs. **to sleep**
- **Ills we have** vs. **others we know not of**

**Effect**: Structures the argument, shows balanced consideration

### 5. Metaphorical Progression
- **Life**: "sea of troubles," "whips and scorns," "weary life"
- **Death**: "sleep," "consummation," "undiscovered country"
- **Thought**: "pale cast," "sicklied o'er"

## Staging Notes

### Physical Staging

**Hamlet's Position**:
- **Alone on stage** (or appears to be)
- **Stationary or pacing**: Depends on interpretation
- **Inward focus**: Not addressing anyone, lost in thought

**Hidden Observers**:
- **Claudius and Polonius**: Behind arras or in alcove
- **Dramatic irony**: Audience knows they're there, Hamlet doesn't (or pretends not to)

**Ophelia's Entrance**:
- **Line 33**: "Soft you now! The fair Ophelia!"
- **Sudden shift**: From philosophical to social mode
- **Question**: Does Hamlet know he's being watched?

### Performance Notes

**Tone**:
- **Opening**: Calm, philosophical, measured
- **Middle**: Building intensity, cataloging suffering
- **Climax**: "Thus conscience does make cowards of us all"
- **Shift**: Sudden change when noticing Ophelia

**Pacing**:
- **Slow, deliberate**: Thinking through each point
- **Pauses**: After questions, before conclusions
- **Acceleration**: During catalog of sufferings (lines 15-19)

**Volume**:
- **Moderate**: Speaking to self, not projecting
- **Variation**: Emphasis on key words (DIE, SLEEP, DREAM, RUB)

**Gestures**:
- **Minimal**: Inward focus, not performative
- **Possible**: Hand to head (thinking), looking at dagger ("bare bodkin")
- **Line 33**: Sudden awareness, turn toward Ophelia

## Thematic Elements

### 1. Existence vs. Non-Existence
The fundamental question: Is it better to exist (with suffering) or not exist?

### 2. Action vs. Inaction
**Hamlet's central problem**: Thinking prevents doing. "Conscience does make cowards of us all."

### 3. Knowledge vs. Ignorance
**Fear of the unknown** (afterlife) keeps us in known suffering. "The undiscovered country."

### 4. Suffering and Endurance
Life is full of pain, but we endure it rather than face the unknown of death.

### 5. The Paralysis of Thought
**Overthinking** prevents action. "The native hue of resolution / Is sicklied o'er with the pale cast of thought."

## Writing Lessons

### 1. The Power of Simple Opening
"To be, or not to be" - six monosyllables, profound question. Simplicity can be powerful.

### 2. Extended Metaphor
Death as sleep is developed throughout: sleep → dream → undiscovered country. Sustain your metaphors.

### 3. Concrete Details Ground Abstraction
"Bare bodkin," "fardels," "grunt and sweat" make philosophical questions tangible.

### 4. Rhetorical Questions Engage
Questions invite the audience to think along with the character.

### 5. Lists Create Weight
The catalog of sufferings (lines 15-19) accumulates emotional and logical force.

### 6. Antithesis Structures Thought
Binary oppositions (be/not be, suffer/take arms) create clear argumentative structure.

### 7. Metrical Variation Marks Emotion
Irregular meter (line 5, line 13) signals emotional intensity or dramatic pause.

### 8. The Turn/Shift
Line 33's sudden shift to Ophelia shows how external reality interrupts internal thought.

## Historical and Critical Context

### Philosophical Influences
- **Stoicism**: Enduring suffering with fortitude
- **Christianity**: Suicide as sin, fear of damnation
- **Renaissance Humanism**: Individual consciousness, self-examination

### Famous Interpretations
- **Laurence Olivier** (1948): Introspective, melancholic
- **Kenneth Branagh** (1996): Watched by Claudius via two-way mirror
- **David Tennant** (2009): Energetic, almost manic

### Critical Debates
- **Is Hamlet suicidal?** Or philosophically exploring the question?
- **Does he know he's being watched?** Some productions suggest he does.
- **What is "the rub"?** The obstacle, the difficulty - fear of afterlife.

## Practice Exercise

**Write a philosophical soliloquy**:
1. Start with a simple, profound question (6-10 words)
2. Explore one side of the question (5-7 lines)
3. Introduce a complication or fear (2-3 lines)
4. Explore the opposite side (5-7 lines)
5. Reach a conclusion (2-3 lines)
6. Include a sudden shift or interruption

**Maintain**:
- Iambic pentameter with strategic variations
- Mix abstract and concrete language
- Use extended metaphor
- Include rhetorical questions
- Build through repetition and lists

**Possible topics**:
- To speak or stay silent
- To forgive or seek revenge
- To pursue ambition or accept contentment
- To love or protect oneself
